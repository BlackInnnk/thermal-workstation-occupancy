Heat in. Insight out.

Four small pieces work together on the edge — nothing leaves the room except a status file and a low-resolution preview.

Sees heat, not people.

A FLIR Lepton radiometric camera captures an 80×60 thermal frame — designed for coarse presence and hot-tool sensing rather than identifying individuals.

Thinks at the edge.

Every frame is processed locally on a Raspberry Pi. When public viewing is enabled, only current states and a low-resolution thermal preview updated every 30 seconds are published.

Learns occupancy.

A compact neural network, trained on labelled lab recordings, classifies each frame as occupied or free in real time.

Watches the heat.

Rule-based temporal logic tracks the tool area over time — safe, monitoring, cooling, or unattended-hot — with hysteresis so a safe result requires sustained below-threshold readings.

FLIR Lepton → Raspberry Pi → ML + safety rules → status.json → Live dashboard

No RGB video. No faces.
Just heat.

Occupancy sensing usually means pointing a camera at people. Hot Seat was designed around the opposite idea.

No RGB camera No colour imagery or identifiable facial detail is captured.
No face recognition The model only answers one question: is someone there?
80×60 pixels People appear only as silhouettes of warmth.
No identity tracking Inference stays on-device; only status data and a low-resolution thermal preview are shared.
